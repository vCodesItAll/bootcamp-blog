Week 6 - Sprint 3 - Pseudocode

In the world of software development, pseudocode is a hot topic. Some swear by it, while others cringe at its mention. However, understanding its role and adopting a pragmatic approach can be a game-changer. Pseudocode is like the blueprint for building software. It's the logic and structure you plan before diving into actual coding. Think of it as the architectural sketch before constructing a building. It's the essential first step towards creating robust and maintainable code. The Journey of Pseudocode Pseudocode isn't one-size-fits-all; it adapts to your style. Imagine an application process where you had to pseudocode making a peanut butter and jelly sandwich. What seemed simple was surprisingly intricate. It highlights the depth of detail pseudocode can uncover. The Unfinished Guide Pseudocode is not a finished product; it's an ever-evolving tool. Instead of striving for perfection, consider it a flexible guide. It's like sculpting â€“ you start with a rough block and refine it as you go, revealing the art within. Deconstructing Complexity When you face a puzzling part of your pseudocode, it's time to deconstruct it. Step back, inspect individual pieces, and refine them if necessary. This process untangles complex problems and eases communication with peers. Embracing Human-Centric Design In coding, the goal is to enhance the human experience, not just write code. Pseudocode bridges ideas and code, serving as your daily exercise to plan like an architect before building. It's simple, like sketching with a pencil, and it's your ally in creating software that improves lives.

Week 8 - CLI

I chose privateinvestocat this week because it's black and white and so is the command line for the most part. It also looks cool and mysterious and that's how I feel now that I use the command line primarily for my git commands. I remember clutching to the git command guide during my first week and struggling to make a new branch. I was in constant fear that I couldn't communicate via the command line and I would fall so far behind in my projects. Every time I typed something I braced myself to see errors that would stump me and my teachers. Fast forward to today and I make branches and make commits and pushes in my sleep or very close to it during late night coding sessions. I no longer find the CLI scary and I feel like a cool stereotypical hacker when I use it. Until being prompted now I never considered building a CLI unless my old Python projects count. If I were to build a CLI I would like a simple 8 ball generator that gives you funny prompts and helps you be more decisive. I love finding creative ways to cut decision fatigue. Life's too short for that. And I'm already fatigued enough. I imagine so many things would take a long time to code in the command line without a framework including a web server, a GUI, a CMS, or a game engine with physics.
